Light – Reflection & Refraction

Key laws that govern how light bounces and bends

01 Reflection

Rear-view Mirrors in Cars

Cars use convex mirrors as rear-view mirrors because they give a wider field of view. The image formed is virtual, erect and diminished.

Mirror Formula 1/f = 1/v + 1/u
🚗

A convex mirror with focal length 30 cm gives a wider view — essential for safe driving.

02 Refraction

A Straw Looks Bent in Water

Light travels from water (denser) to air (rarer) and bends away from the normal — making the straw appear bent at the water surface.

Snell's Law n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂
🥤

Refractive index of water ≈ 1.33. Light bends at the air-water interface creating the illusion.

03 Lenses

Magnifying Glass (Convex Lens)

A convex lens converges light rays. When an object is placed within the focal length, it acts as a magnifying glass producing a virtual, erect, and enlarged image.

Magnification m = v / u = h' / h
🔍

Jewellers use convex lenses (f ≈ 5 cm) to inspect gems — the lens is held close to the eye and object.

04 Total Internal Reflection

Optical Fibre & Internet

Light travels through optical fibres by total internal reflection. The fibre core is denser than the cladding, so light bounces repeatedly without escaping.

Critical Angle Condition sin C = n₂ / n₁ (n₁ > n₂)
🌐

Your broadband internet likely travels as light pulses in glass fibres — 100 Gbps+ speeds!

Human Eye & Colourful World

Vision, defects, corrections and atmospheric optics

05 Myopia

Short-sightedness & Spectacles

In myopia, the image forms in front of the retina. A concave lens of suitable power diverges rays so they focus correctly on the retina.

Lens Power P = 1/f (in metres) → unit: Dioptre
👓

A person can see only up to 2 m. Correction lens: P = 1/(-2) = −0.5 D (concave).

06 Dispersion

Rainbow Formation

Raindrops act as tiny prisms. Sunlight undergoes refraction, total internal reflection, and dispersion inside each raindrop, splitting into the spectrum of VIBGYOR.

Dispersion Violet bends most, Red bends least
🌈

Primary rainbow: 40°–42° from anti-solar point. You always see it opposite the sun!

07 Scattering

Why is the Sky Blue?

Rayleigh scattering – shorter (blue) wavelengths are scattered more by atmospheric particles. Our eyes receive more scattered blue light from all directions.

Scattering ∝ 1 / λ⁴ (Rayleigh Law)
🌤️

At sunrise/sunset, light travels further through the atmosphere — blue is scattered away, leaving red/orange.

08 Hypermetropia

Long-sightedness & Convex Lens

In hypermetropia, image forms behind the retina. A convex lens converges rays so they focus on the retina.

Near Point shifted P = +ve Dioptre (convex lens)
📖

Reading glasses are convex lenses. A person with near point at 1 m needs P = 1/0.25 − 1/1 = +3 D.

Electricity

Current, resistance, Ohm's law and power in daily life

09 Ohm's Law

Electric Heater Design

A heater's coil has high resistance. By Ohm's law, V = IR, more resistance → more heat for the same voltage. The coil is made of nichrome (high ρ, high melting point).

Ohm's Law V = I × R
🔥

A 2000 W heater on 220 V draws I = 2000/220 ≈ 9.1 A. Resistance ≈ 24.2 Ω.

10 Series Circuit

Old Christmas Lights

Old fairy lights were wired in series. If one bulb blew, the whole string went out — because breaking one resistor opens the entire circuit.

Series Resistance R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + …
🎄

Modern LEDs use parallel wiring — one fails, others stay on. R_parallel = 1/(1/R₁ + 1/R₂…)

11 Electric Power

Reading Your Electricity Bill

Energy is sold in kilowatt-hours (kWh). One unit = 1 kWh. A 100 W bulb on for 10 hours consumes 1 unit.

Power & Energy P = VI = I²R; E = P × t
💡

A 5-star AC (1500 W) running 8 h/day for 30 days = 360 kWh. At ₹6/unit → ₹2,160 per month.

12 Fuse

Safety Fuse in Your Home

A fuse wire has low melting point and low resistance. When excess current flows, it heats up (P = I²R) and melts, breaking the circuit before damage occurs.

Heating Effect H = I² × R × t (Joule's Law)
🔌

A 5 A fuse melts if current exceeds 5 A — protects expensive appliances from short circuits.

Magnetic Effects of Electric Current

Electromagnets, motors, generators and electromagnetic induction

13 Electromagnet

MRI Machines in Hospitals

MRI scanners use superconducting electromagnets generating fields of 1–3 Tesla. The magnetic field aligns hydrogen protons in the body to create detailed images.

Magnetic Field (Solenoid) B = μ₀ n I
🏥

More turns (n) and more current (I) → stronger B field. That's why MRI coils have thousands of turns.

14 Electric Motor

Ceiling Fan Motor

A current-carrying coil in a magnetic field experiences a force (Fleming's LHR). This rotates the armature — converting electrical energy to mechanical energy.

Force on conductor F = B I L sin θ
🌀

Your ceiling fan, mixer, washing machine, and electric vehicles all use this principle.

15 Electromagnetic Induction

Power Plant Generators

A rotating coil in a magnetic field induces an EMF (Faraday's Law). This is how all electricity is generated — coal, hydro, wind, and nuclear plants.

Faraday's Law ε = −N × ΔΦ/Δt
🏭

A 500 MW generator at a power plant rotates at 50 Hz — producing 50 cycles/sec of AC current.

16 AC vs DC

Why AC is Used for Transmission

AC voltage can be stepped up using transformers to reduce I²R losses during long-distance transmission. DC cannot be transformed — so AC is preferred nationally.

Transformer Ratio V_s/V_p = N_s/N_p
🗼

Power leaves plants at 220 kV, travels to cities, is stepped down to 11 kV then 220 V for homes.

Sources of Energy

Conventional and non-conventional energy with real applications

17 Solar Energy

Solar Panels on Rooftops

Photovoltaic cells (solar cells) convert sunlight directly to electricity using the photoelectric effect. Connected in series/parallel they form solar panels.

Solar Power P = η × A × I_solar
☀️

A 1 kW rooftop system generates ~4–5 units/day in India — saving ₹24–30 daily at ₹6/unit.

18 Wind Energy

Wind Turbines

Wind (kinetic energy) spins turbine blades which rotate a generator — converting KE to electrical energy. India is world's 4th largest wind power producer.

Wind Power P = ½ ρ A v³
💨

Doubling wind speed → 8× more power! That's why turbines are placed on hills and coasts.

19 Hydropower

Bhakra Nangal Dam

Water stored at height has gravitational PE. Released through penstocks, it converts to KE, spinning turbines to generate electricity. Zero fuel cost!

Potential Energy PE = mgh
🌊

Bhakra Dam (226 m high) generates 1325 MW — powering millions of homes in Punjab, Haryana, HP.

20 Nuclear Energy

Tarapur Atomic Power Station

Nuclear fission of Uranium-235: one neutron splits a U-235 nucleus releasing ~200 MeV of energy and 2–3 more neutrons (chain reaction).

Mass-Energy Relation E = mc²
☢️

1 kg of U-235 releases energy equivalent to burning 3,000 tonnes of coal — with zero CO₂ emissions.
